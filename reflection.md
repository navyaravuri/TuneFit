# Reflection

## Limitations and Biases

The most significant structural bias in TuneFit is genre anchoring. In a catalog where 13 of 15 genres have exactly one representative song, the +2.0 genre bonus permanently locks that single song into first place regardless of how poorly its audio features actually match what the user wants. Testing confirmed this directly: a user asking for high-energy classical music received Midnight Sonata — a quiet, slow piano piece with energy=0.22 — as their top result, because the genre label alone outweighed five badly-scored audio features. Blackout Riff, which matched the energy and tempo targets almost perfectly, lost by just 0.06 points because it had no genre bonus to draw from. I ran weight experiments to fix this — doubling the energy weight and halving the genre weight resolved that case, but it broke the rock profiles, where Storm Runner stopped winning for users who explicitly asked for rock. Turning one dial fixed one thing and broke another. The real limitation is 18 songs; the weights are a reasonable compromise given the data constraints, but the catalog is too small for the scoring system to behave reliably across all user types.

A second bias is in the mood groups. The groupings — chill/relaxed/focused, happy/euphoric/energetic, sad/melancholic/moody — are hand-coded based on intuition, not research, and reflect one developer's understanding of emotional categories. They may not generalize across cultures or personal associations. TuneFit 2.0 adds a third layer of bias at the LLM step: the same ambiguous input ("something electronic and relaxing") can produce different extracted genres across runs — sometimes `lofi`, sometimes `ambient` — making the final recommendations non-deterministic even though the scoring engine itself is fully deterministic.

## Misuse Potential

TuneFit 2.0 is a prototype and would cause real harm as a production system. Users with musical preferences outside the catalog's narrow scope — reggae, gospel, bhangra, afrobeat — consistently receive mismatched recommendations with no indication that their genre preference went completely unmet. In TuneFit 1.0, this meant scores still displayed out of 9.0 even though the genre bonus could never fire, making results appear weaker than expected without any explanation. TuneFit 2.0 added explicit confidence warnings for this case, but those warnings only fire when the system detects the gap — they don't catch cases where the LLM silently maps an unfamiliar genre to something nearby in the catalog.

The most responsible safeguard would be disclosing catalog scope and geographic bias to users before they receive results, and capping confidence scores to reflect the actual ceiling when the genre bonus is unavailable. A system that presents itself as more general-purpose than it is causes a specific kind of harm: users make decisions based on recommendations that were structurally impossible to get right.

## What Surprised Me During Testing

The adversarial conflicting-preferences test surprised me most. I designed it specifically to stress-test the system: "give me something super high energy but also very acoustic and peaceful" is contradictory by design, and I expected it to produce low confidence. Instead, it consistently returned medium confidence. When I inspected the reasoning trace, the LLM had resolved the contradiction before it ever reached the scoring engine — producing `target_acousticness=0.5` as a compromise rather than pushing both dimensions to their extremes. The scoring engine never saw the conflict I intended to test.

This was the most important thing I learned from testing: the LLM is not a transparent pass-through. It interprets and smooths inputs in ways that can quietly neutralize the adversarial conditions you're trying to evaluate. A system you think you're stress-testing may be defanging your own tests. The other surprise was how different LLM failures feel compared to code failures. In TuneFit 1.0, every bug was loud — a crash, a wrong number, an empty list. In TuneFit 2.0, the model could extract the wrong genre, the scoring engine would run correctly, the confidence would look fine, and the results would be slightly off in a way that only registered if you already knew the expected answer. Quiet failures are harder to catch than loud ones.

## AI Collaboration

I used Claude throughout this project for architecture planning, implementation, and writing.

**One instance where the AI gave a helpful suggestion:** When designing the four-tool pipeline, I originally planned to combine confidence evaluation and response formatting into a single step — evaluate how well the results matched and then immediately write the summary. Claude suggested separating them: keep `evaluate_confidence` as a standalone, LLM-free tool that runs before the formatting call. This turned out to be the right decision. It meant the confidence logic could be unit-tested without any LLM calls, the warning system could be validated independently, and the reasoning trace in the UI could show confidence as a distinct step rather than a side effect of response generation. The separation made the whole pipeline more inspectable.

**One instance where the AI's suggestion was flawed:** Claude recommended using Streamlit's built-in `st.progress()` component for the score bars in the song cards. This worked functionally, but gave no control over bar color — Streamlit's default styling produced a color that clashed with the amber design theme and visually implied a warning state for high-scoring songs, which was the opposite of the intended effect. I had to replace the component entirely with custom HTML and a CSS gradient bar. The suggestion wasn't wrong about what `st.progress()` does, but it didn't account for the styling constraints of the design, which would have been obvious to flag if the full visual context had been part of the conversation.
